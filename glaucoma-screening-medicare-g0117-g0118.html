Medicare Glaucoma Screening: Who Qualifies, How Often, and What to Document

Medicare covers glaucoma screening for high-risk patients — but the definition of "high-risk" is more specific than most independent OD practices realize. Billing G0117 or G0118 for patients who don't meet CMS's documented eligibility criteria is one of the most common audit triggers in optometry billing.

This guide covers exactly who qualifies for Medicare glaucoma screening, how often it can be billed, what the chart must document, and where the most common billing errors occur.

The Two Glaucoma Screening Codes

Medicare covers glaucoma screening under two HCPCS codes:

  • G0117 — Glaucoma screening for high-risk patients furnished by an optometrist or ophthalmologist
  • G0118 — Glaucoma screening for high-risk patients furnished under the direct supervision of an optometrist or ophthalmologist

The distinction between G0117 and G0118 is who performs the screening — G0117 is for screenings performed directly by the OD or MD, while G0118 covers screenings performed by a trained technician under direct supervision. Most independent OD practices bill G0117.

Who Qualifies: The High-Risk Criteria

CMS defines "high-risk" for glaucoma screening purposes as patients who meet at least one of the following criteria:

  • Diabetes mellitus (any type)
  • Family history of glaucoma
  • African American patients age 50 and older
  • Hispanic patients age 65 and older

"Medicare glaucoma screening is not a universal benefit. It applies only to patients who meet at least one of four specific high-risk criteria — and that eligibility must be documented in the chart."

This is where most billing errors occur. ODs screen for glaucoma as part of routine comprehensive exams and bill G0117 without verifying that the patient meets one of these four criteria. A patient who is 60 years old with no diabetes, no family history of glaucoma, and is not African American or Hispanic does not qualify for Medicare-covered glaucoma screening — regardless of their IOP or cup-to-disc ratio.

Frequency Limitations

Medicare covers glaucoma screening once every 12 months for eligible high-risk patients. Billing G0117 or G0118 more frequently than once per year will result in denial unless there is documented medical necessity for additional monitoring — in which case the appropriate eye exam code (92012/92014) should be used instead, not the screening code.

Frequency Rule: G0117/G0118 — once per 12 months per eligible beneficiary
Common Error: Billing a glaucoma screening code at a follow-up visit for a patient already diagnosed with glaucoma. Screening codes are for detection, not management. Use 92012/92014 for glaucoma management visits.

What the Chart Must Document

To support a G0117 or G0118 claim, the chart must clearly document:

  • The specific high-risk criterion the patient meets (e.g., "Patient has Type 2 diabetes mellitus" or "Patient reports family history of glaucoma — father diagnosed age 58")
  • The screening examination performed (tonometry, ophthalmoscopy, or other appropriate test)
  • The findings of the screening
  • The date of the last glaucoma screening if previously performed

The Diagnosis Code Connection

The ICD-10 diagnosis code on the claim must reflect the high-risk condition, not glaucoma itself. If you're billing G0117 for a diabetic patient, the primary diagnosis should be the diabetes code (E11.xx), not a glaucoma code. If glaucoma has already been diagnosed, you should be managing it with an eye exam code, not screening for it.

Screening vs. Management: A Critical Distinction

One of the most common billing errors in this area is using glaucoma screening codes for patients who already have a glaucoma diagnosis. The G codes are for screening — detecting glaucoma in high-risk patients who haven't been diagnosed. Once glaucoma is diagnosed, those patients should be billed under the appropriate eye exam codes (92012/92014) with glaucoma management as the documented purpose of the visit.

  • G0117/G0118 — Screening: patient at high risk, no current glaucoma diagnosis
  • 92012/92014 — Management: patient has diagnosed glaucoma, returning for monitoring and treatment

Practical Steps to Reduce Audit Exposure

The most effective way to protect your practice on glaucoma screening codes is to implement a simple eligibility check before billing G0117 or G0118 on any claim:

  • Confirm the patient meets at least one CMS high-risk criterion
  • Document the specific criterion in the chart note
  • Verify the last glaucoma screening date is more than 12 months ago
  • Confirm the patient does not have an existing glaucoma diagnosis
  • Use the appropriate ICD-10 code reflecting the high-risk condition, not glaucoma

A simple EHR template prompt or billing checklist can catch these errors before the claim goes out — and is far less expensive than a MAC audit or recoupment demand.
