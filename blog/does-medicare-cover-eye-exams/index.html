Does Medicare Cover Eye Exams? What's Covered and What's Not

By OptometristBillingClarity · April 2026 · 9 min read

The answer depends entirely on what kind of eye exam you're talking about. Medicare Part B covers medical eye exams — exams to diagnose or manage eye diseases like glaucoma, cataracts, macular degeneration, and diabetic retinopathy. Medicare does NOT cover routine vision exams — exams solely to determine your glasses or contact lens prescription.

This distinction is the single most important concept in Medicare eye care billing, and getting it wrong results in denied claims, patient billing confusion, and audit risk.

What Medicare Covers

Medical Eye Exams (Covered)

Medicare Part B covers comprehensive eye exams when the purpose is to evaluate, diagnose, or manage a medical eye condition. These are billed using medical evaluation CPT codes (92002, 92004, 92012, 92014). Medicare pays 80% of the allowed amount after the Part B deductible, and the patient pays 20% coinsurance. Covered conditions include glaucoma and glaucoma suspect, cataracts, macular degeneration (AMD), diabetic retinopathy, dry eye disease, corneal conditions, and any medical complaint related to the eyes (pain, redness, vision changes, floaters, flashes).

Glaucoma Screening (Covered for High-Risk Patients)

Medicare covers an annual glaucoma screening for patients who are at high risk — including those with diabetes, a family history of glaucoma, African Americans age 50+, and Hispanic Americans age 65+. This is billed using screening codes G0117 (by a non-physician) or G0118 (by a physician/optometrist).

Diabetic Eye Exams (Covered)

Medicare covers annual dilated eye exams for patients with diabetes. These exams specifically evaluate for diabetic retinopathy and other diabetic eye complications. They are billed as medical eye exams under the standard evaluation codes, with the diabetes diagnosis supporting medical necessity.

What Medicare Does NOT Cover

  • Routine eye exams for glasses — An exam solely to update a glasses prescription, with no medical condition being evaluated, is not covered by Medicare Part B
  • Routine refractions — The refraction (determining glasses prescription) is not covered by Medicare even during a medical exam. It must be billed separately to the patient (or to vision insurance if they have it)
  • Eyeglasses — Medicare does not cover glasses except after cataract surgery with an intraocular lens implant (one pair of standard frames and lenses)
  • Contact lenses — Not covered except in rare medical circumstances (post-cataract surgery, keratoconus)
  • Vision therapy and orthoptics — Not covered by Medicare Part B

The refraction problem: Even during a Medicare-covered medical eye exam, the refraction component (checking glasses prescription) is not covered. Most OD practices charge patients $25-$45 for the refraction as a separate out-of-pocket charge. Patients should be informed of this charge before the exam. Some practices absorb the refraction cost as a courtesy, but this must be applied consistently — selective waiver can create compliance issues.

How the Exam Gets Classified

Whether a visit is billed as a medical exam (covered) or routine exam (not covered) is determined by the reason for the visit, not what happens during the exam. If a patient comes in complaining of blurry vision and the OD discovers they simply need a new glasses prescription with no medical findings, the exam may still be billed as medical if the evaluation was conducted to rule out medical causes of the vision change. Conversely, if a patient specifically requests a glasses check with no medical complaints and the OD finds mild cataracts incidentally, the visit is still routine — the incidental finding can be noted and followed up at a separate medical visit.

The documentation must clearly establish the medical purpose of the visit. Chief complaint, medical history review, and clinical decision-making must all support the medical billing level selected.

Bifurcated Visits (Split Billing)

When a patient visit involves both medical and routine components — for example, a glaucoma evaluation plus a glasses prescription update — the visit can be "bifurcated" (split). The medical portion is billed to Medicare Part B, and the routine/refraction portion is billed to the patient or their vision insurance. This requires clear documentation separating the medical and routine components of the visit, separate billing for each component with appropriate codes and diagnoses, and communication with the patient about which portions are covered by Medicare and which are out-of-pocket.

How Much Does Medicare Pay?

Medicare pays 80% of the allowed amount for medical eye exams after the Part B deductible. Approximate 2026 allowed amounts by code are 92012 (established, intermediate) at $75-$95, 92014 (established, comprehensive) at $110-$140, 92002 (new patient, intermediate) at $90-$110, and 92004 (new patient, comprehensive) at $140-$175. The patient pays 20% coinsurance on these amounts. Patients with Medigap supplemental coverage may have their coinsurance covered.

Medicare Advantage Plans

Many Medicare Advantage (Part C) plans offer expanded vision benefits beyond original Medicare — including routine eye exams, glasses allowances, and contact lens coverage. If your patient has a Medicare Advantage plan, verify the specific plan's vision benefits. Do not assume they have the same coverage as original Medicare.

The Bottom Line

Medicare covers eye exams when there's a medical reason — a disease to diagnose, monitor, or manage. It does not cover routine exams for glasses prescriptions. For optometrists, the key is documenting the medical purpose of every Medicare visit clearly, managing patient expectations about what's covered (especially the refraction charge), and mastering bifurcated billing for visits that involve both medical and routine components.
