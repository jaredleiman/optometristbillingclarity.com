How Much Does an Eye Exam Cost? Complete Price Breakdown

By OptometristBillingClarity · April 2026 · 8 min read

An eye exam in the United States costs between $50 and $250, with the average falling around $100-$150 for a comprehensive exam at a private practice. Your actual cost depends on three main factors: where you go, what type of exam you get, and how you pay.

Cost by Provider Type

Retail optical chains (Walmart, Costco, America's Best) offer the lowest prices at $50-$100, making them accessible for patients on tight budgets. The trade-off is typically shorter appointment times and a focus on refraction rather than comprehensive medical evaluation. Independent optometry practices charge $100-$200 for comprehensive exams, with the higher price reflecting longer appointments, more thorough evaluation, and the ability to diagnose and manage medical eye conditions. Ophthalmology practices charge $150-$350, reflecting the physician's medical and surgical expertise and the ability to manage complex eye diseases.

Cost by Exam Type

A routine vision exam (primarily for glasses/contacts prescription) runs $50-$125. A comprehensive medical eye exam (evaluating eye health, diagnosing conditions) costs $100-$250. A contact lens exam (fitting and evaluation in addition to the comprehensive exam) adds $50-$150 to the base exam cost. Specialty exams like pediatric evaluations, low vision assessments, or sports vision testing range from $150-$300.

Cost With Insurance

Vision Insurance

With vision insurance (VSP, EyeMed, Davis Vision), most plans cover one routine eye exam per year with a copay of $10-$25. The insurer pays the remainder directly to the provider. Vision insurance typically does not cover medical eye exams — those go through your health insurance.

Health Insurance

Medical eye exams (for eye diseases or medical complaints) are covered under health insurance, not vision insurance. With health insurance, you pay your plan's specialist copay ($25-$50) or coinsurance after your deductible is met. Most plans cover medical eye exams without a visit limit as long as they're medically necessary.

Medicare

Medicare Part B covers medical eye exams at 80% after the $257 deductible. The patient pays 20% coinsurance, typically $15-$30 per medical eye exam. Medicare does not cover routine eye exams for glasses. See our Medicare eye exam coverage guide for details.

Additional Costs to Expect

Beyond the base exam fee, you may encounter charges for refraction ($25-$45, often not covered by Medicare or some health plans), retinal imaging/OCT ($25-$75), visual field testing ($30-$75), dilation ($0-$25, sometimes included), and contact lens fitting ($50-$150, separate from exam). Always ask the office what's included in the quoted exam price and what might be extra before your appointment.

Price transparency tip: Call the office before your appointment and ask: "What is the total cost of a comprehensive eye exam for a self-pay patient, including refraction and any standard testing?" This gives you the all-in price rather than a base price that excludes common add-ons.

Ways to Save

  • Ask for self-pay discounts — Most private practices offer 15-30% off for cash payment at the time of service
  • Use HSA/FSA funds — Eye exams are eligible expenses, saving you 20-35% through pre-tax dollars
  • Compare providers — Price differences of 50-100% exist between practices in the same area
  • Check if your exam qualifies as medical — If you have any eye symptoms or conditions, the exam may be covered by health insurance rather than requiring out-of-pocket payment
  • Consider optometry school clinics — Supervised student exams at $25-$50 are a legitimate option for routine care

For OD Practice Owners: Pricing Your Exams

Your exam pricing strategy affects patient acquisition, revenue per visit, and perceived practice positioning. Consider whether your pricing positions you as premium (full-service medical eye care), competitive (matching or beating local private practice averages), or value-oriented (competing with retail chains on price). Most successful independent practices price in the $100-$175 range for comprehensive exams, offer a visible cash-pay discount, and differentiate on thoroughness and medical expertise rather than trying to match retail chain prices.

The Bottom Line

Eye exam costs are predictable and manageable — whether you pay out of pocket or use insurance. The most important variable is the type of exam you need. If you have medical eye concerns, you may have more coverage than you think through your health insurance. If you just need a glasses check, vision insurance or a cash-pay visit to a competitively priced provider keeps costs reasonable.
