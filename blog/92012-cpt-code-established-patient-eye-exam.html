Does Medicare Cover Glasses? What Beneficiaries Need to Know

By OptometristBillingClarity · April 2026 · 7 min read

Medicare does not cover routine eyeglasses — with one important exception. After cataract surgery with an intraocular lens implant, Medicare Part B pays for one pair of standard frames and lenses (or contact lenses instead). Outside of this post-cataract exception, glasses, frames, lenses, and contact lenses are not a covered Medicare Part B benefit.

The Post-Cataract Surgery Exception

This is the only situation where original Medicare covers corrective lenses. After cataract surgery that includes IOL implantation, Medicare covers one pair of eyeglasses or one set of contact lenses. Coverage includes standard frames from a Medicare-enrolled supplier, prescription lenses (single vision, bifocal, or trifocal), or contact lenses as an alternative to glasses. Medicare pays 80% of the approved amount after the Part B deductible, and the patient pays 20% coinsurance.

Important limitations: upgrades beyond standard frames and lenses (progressive lenses, photochromic lenses, anti-reflective coating, designer frames) are not covered. The patient can choose upgrades but must pay the additional cost out of pocket. The glasses must be obtained from a Medicare-enrolled supplier — not all optical shops are enrolled.

What Medicare Does NOT Cover

  • Routine eyeglasses for refractive error (nearsightedness, farsightedness, astigmatism, presbyopia)
  • Contact lenses except post-cataract or rare medical conditions
  • Lens upgrades beyond standard options
  • Sunglasses — not covered under any circumstance
  • Routine eye exams solely for determining glasses prescription

Medicare Advantage Vision Benefits

Many Medicare Advantage (Part C) plans offer vision benefits that original Medicare does not, including annual routine eye exams, glasses allowances (typically $100-$300 per year), and contact lens allowances. These supplemental vision benefits are one of the primary reasons beneficiaries choose Medicare Advantage. If your patient has an MA plan, always verify their specific vision benefits rather than assuming original Medicare rules apply.

Affordable Alternatives for Medicare Beneficiaries

  • Supplemental vision insurance — Plans from VSP or EyeMed cost $10-$20/month and typically include a glasses allowance
  • Online eyewear retailers — Zenni, EyeBuyDirect, and similar sites offer prescription glasses starting at $15-$50
  • Charitable programs — Lions Club and New Eyes for the Needy provide free glasses for qualifying individuals
  • Warehouse clubs — Costco and Sam's Club optical departments offer competitive pricing on glasses

For ODs: When Medicare patients need glasses but don't qualify for the post-cataract exception, clearly explain that Medicare doesn't cover them. Offer to check whether they have supplemental vision insurance, provide your cash-pay pricing, or refer to affordable options. This transparency builds trust and prevents billing confusion.

The Bottom Line

Medicare's glasses coverage is limited to one specific situation: post-cataract surgery. For everything else, beneficiaries need supplemental vision insurance, Medicare Advantage with vision benefits, or out-of-pocket purchasing. Knowing this distinction — and communicating it clearly to patients — is essential for both ODs and the Medicare beneficiaries they serve.

CPT Code 92012: Established Patient Intermediate Eye Exam Billing Guide

By OptometristBillingClarity · April 2026 · 7 min read

CPT 92012 is the intermediate ophthalmological examination code for established patients. It covers focused medical eye evaluations that don't require the full comprehensive assessment of a 92014. Understanding when 92012 is the correct code — rather than upcoding to 92014 or undercoding a visit that warrants comprehensive evaluation — is one of the most important billing decisions in daily optometric practice.

When to Use 92012

92012 is appropriate for established patient visits that involve evaluation of a new or existing condition that does not require comprehensive examination. Common scenarios include follow-up on a previously diagnosed condition (dry eye check, IOP recheck for glaucoma), evaluation of a new but focused complaint (red eye, foreign body sensation, flashes/floaters), contact lens problem visits with medical component, and medication follow-up visits (checking response to a prescribed eye drop).

92012 vs. 92014: Making the Right Call

The distinction comes down to examination scope. 92012 evaluates one or a few aspects of the visual system relevant to the presenting condition. 92014 evaluates the complete visual system comprehensively. If a patient presents for a dry eye follow-up and you assess the anterior segment, tear film, and lid margins without performing dilated fundus exam or full posterior segment evaluation, that's 92012. If the same patient also needs annual glaucoma monitoring and you perform a full dilated exam with IOP, visual fields, and OCT, that's 92014.

The documentation test: If your note documents findings in both the anterior AND posterior segments with dilated examination, it likely supports 92014. If your note focuses on one segment or one condition without full-system evaluation, 92012 is the appropriate code. Let the documentation drive the code — not the other way around.

Documentation Requirements

92012 documentation must include focused history relevant to the presenting condition, examination of the affected area(s) with objective findings, medical decision-making interpreting findings and determining management, and a plan with treatment, follow-up, or referral as appropriate. The documentation does not need to cover every element of a comprehensive exam — but it must be thorough enough for the condition being evaluated.

Medicare Reimbursement

Medicare allows approximately $75-$95 for 92012, compared to $110-$140 for 92014. The $30-$45 difference per visit adds up, which creates a financial incentive to upcode to 92014. Resist this — consistent upcoding is one of the most common audit findings in optometry and results in repayment demands plus potential penalties.

Common Billing Errors

  • Always billing 92014 and never 92012 — A practice that never bills 92012 is signaling to auditors that every visit is comprehensive, which is clinically unrealistic
  • Billing 92012 for routine-only visits — If there's no medical condition being evaluated, 92012 is not appropriate. Routine vision exams are billed differently.
  • Undercoding 92014 visits as 92012 — If you performed and documented a comprehensive evaluation, bill 92014. Undercoding costs revenue unnecessarily.

The Bottom Line

92012 is the correct code for most follow-up visits and focused evaluations in optometry. A healthy practice billing pattern includes a mix of both 92012 and 92014 — not exclusively one or the other. Let the clinical situation and your documentation determine the code, and you'll be both accurately reimbursed and audit-ready.
