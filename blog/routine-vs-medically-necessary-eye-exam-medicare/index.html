Routine vs. Medically Necessary Eye Exams: How to Split Bill Medicare Correctly

Every independent OD practice sees patients who arrive for what appears to be a routine exam but also have medical conditions that require evaluation. The question of how to bill these visits — or whether to split them — is one of the most nuanced compliance challenges in optometry Medicare billing.

Getting it wrong in either direction creates exposure. Billing the entire visit to Medicare when part of it was routine is overbilling. Billing the entire visit to a vision plan when part of it was medically necessary means leaving legitimate Medicare revenue uncollected — and potentially violating participation agreements.

The Core Principle: Medicare Covers Medical, Not Routine

Medicare Part B covers medically necessary eye care. It explicitly excludes routine vision exams — those performed to determine the need for corrective lenses or to update a glasses prescription. When a patient visit includes both medical and routine components, the visit must be bifurcated: the medical portion billed to Medicare, and the routine portion billed elsewhere (vision plan, patient self-pay).

"A visit that includes both a medical eye exam and a refraction is not entirely covered by Medicare. The refraction is always routine — Medicare does not cover refractions under any circumstances."

What Is Always Routine (Never Medicare-Covered)

Regardless of the patient's medical conditions, these services are never covered by Medicare Part B:

  • Refraction (the determination of glasses or contact lens prescription)
  • Contact lens fittings
  • Routine vision checks with no medical complaint or diagnosis
  • Glasses or contact lens prescriptions as the primary purpose of the visit

When Split Billing Is Required

Split billing is required when a single patient visit includes both a medically necessary eye exam component and a routine component. The most common scenario is a patient with a medical eye condition (glaucoma, diabetic retinopathy, macular degeneration) who also wants a new glasses prescription.

In this scenario, the medical exam is billable to Medicare Part B (using 92004/92014 with the appropriate medical diagnosis). The refraction is billed separately — either to a vision plan if the patient has one, or as a patient self-pay service.

The ABN Requirement

When a service will not be covered by Medicare and the patient will be responsible for payment, an Advance Beneficiary Notice of Noncoverage (ABN) is required. For split billing scenarios, the ABN should be given to the patient before the routine portion of the visit to document that they understand Medicare will not cover it and they will be responsible for that cost.

ABN Timing: Must be given BEFORE the service is performed — not after
ABN Content: Must specify which service won't be covered and the estimated cost
ABN Signature: Patient must sign — unsigned ABNs are not valid
ABN Retention: Keep signed ABNs in the patient file for at least 4 years

When an ABN Is Not Required

If a service is categorically excluded from Medicare coverage (like a refraction), a standard ABN is not technically required — but it is still best practice to inform patients in writing that the service is not covered and obtain their agreement to pay. Many practices use a standard "Medicare Vision Exclusion" notice for refractions.

How to Structure the Claim

When split billing a visit with both medical and routine components:

  • Bill Medicare Part B for the medical exam component using 92004 or 92014 with the medical diagnosis code
  • Do not include the refraction on the Medicare claim
  • Bill the refraction separately to the vision plan using the appropriate vision plan code, or to the patient directly
  • Document both components separately in the chart note
  • Retain the signed ABN or vision exclusion notice

The Most Common Split Billing Errors

  • Billing the entire visit to Medicare including a refraction — Medicare will deny the refraction and may flag the pattern for audit
  • Billing a lower-level code to Medicare to "account for" the routine portion — the Medicare claim should reflect only the medical services, coded correctly
  • Not giving the ABN before service — retroactive ABNs are not valid; patients cannot be billed for services they weren't informed about in advance
  • Using refractive error as the primary diagnosis on the Medicare claim — this signals routine care, not medical necessity, and will likely result in denial

Practical Documentation Approach

The cleanest approach is to structure the chart note with clearly separated sections for the medical exam and the routine/refractive components. This makes it immediately clear to any auditor that the visit had two distinct components billed to different payers for different services.

Some EHR systems support separate encounter templates for medical versus routine exam components. If yours does, use them — it creates a natural documentation structure that aligns with the billing bifurcation.
