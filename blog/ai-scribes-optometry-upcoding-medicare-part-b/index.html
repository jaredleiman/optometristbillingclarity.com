AI Scribes Are Inflating Optometry Codes — What Medicare Part B Auditors Are Watching

Blue Cross Blue Shield Association released a report in March 2026 documenting more than $2 billion in AI-driven hospital upcoding. The story didn't get much attention in the optometry trade press, but it should have. The same ambient-listening tools driving inpatient upcoding are now in optometry exam lanes, and the audit logic that catches hospitals catches independent ODs just as easily.

The BCBS Finding in Plain English

BCBS analyzed de-identified claims data across tens of thousands of admissions and found hospitals using AI scribes were coding significantly more complex diagnoses than they were treating. The headline example — maternity admissions coded for acute posthemorrhagic anemia without the transfusion that should follow — produced $22 million in excess maternity spending at the studied facilities alone. National exposure was estimated at $2.3 billion across inpatient and outpatient.

Optometry doesn't have a single headline diagnosis like that, but it has something auditors love more: a small number of high-volume codes with clear medical necessity rules.

Where AI Scribes Drift in Optometry

The risk is concentrated in four places independent ODs already know:

  • 92014 vs 92012. The comprehensive/intermediate distinction hinges on specific elements — history, general medical observation, external and ophthalmoscopic exam, initiation of diagnostic and treatment programs. AI scribes that pull from exam templates tend to auto-populate "comprehensive" language regardless of what was actually performed.
  • 92004 new patient comprehensive. Medicare requires the exam to be for a diagnosis and treatment plan beyond refractive error. Scribe-generated notes that include the phrase "evaluation and treatment plan initiated" without a corresponding medical condition drive a textbook denial pattern.
  • 99213/99214 for medical eye visits. Some ODs bill E/M codes instead of ophthalmological services, and the complexity level is where AI scribes inflate fastest. A 99213 encounter documented as 99214 because the AI captured "diabetes" from patient history — without the diabetes actually driving the medical decision-making — is the exact pattern CERT and RAC reviewers target.
  • Dilated fundus exam attestation. G0117/G0118 and the dilated component of 92014 require documentation that dilation actually occurred and what was observed. AI notes that include "fundus normal" without any indication of dilation are already appearing in audit samples.

The Pattern That Gets You Flagged

Medicare contractors don't audit individual claims. They audit patterns. The flag is a sudden shift in your billing mix — a higher percentage of 92014s than your specialty peers, a climbing proportion of 99214s, a rise in diabetic retinopathy diagnoses without a corresponding rise in A1C documentation. Payer AI spots these shifts within 60-90 days.

BCBS's own analysis of the top 10% of high-growth hospitals found those hospitals were driving the overwhelming majority of coding intensity increases. For independent ODs, the equivalent is this: the practices that integrated AI scribes in 2025 and didn't change their review workflow are showing coding patterns their unassisted peers aren't. Those practices will be the first to see prepayment review, medical record requests, and CERT samples in 2026.

What to Check in Your Practice This Week

  1. Pull your billing mix for the last 90 days. What percentage of your established patient visits are 92014 vs 92012? If it's above 70/30, pull 10 charts and read the AI-drafted notes against CMS's comprehensive exam definition.
  2. Check every 99214 billed this quarter. Is the medical decision-making documentation moderate or high complexity in the actual note body, or did the AI insert "moderate complexity" as a header?
  3. Review your dilation documentation. Does each dilated exam include the dilating agent used, the time waited, and specific fundus findings?
  4. Look at diabetic retinopathy diagnoses (E11.319, E11.329, etc.). Is there a linked diabetes diagnosis (E11.9) in the same claim, and is there a severity determination in the chart?

The Bottom Line

AI scribes are not the enemy. Unreviewed AI scribe output is. The BCBS findings are an early warning that payer audit AI is now sensitive enough to detect coding drift within a quarter. Independent optometry practices that treat the scribe's output as a starting draft — and the provider's final note as a personal attestation — will be fine. Practices that don't will see their audit risk climb well before their revenue does.
