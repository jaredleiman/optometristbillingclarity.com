Does Medicare Cover Glasses? What Beneficiaries Need to Know

By OptometristBillingClarity · April 2026 · 7 min read

Medicare does not cover routine eyeglasses — with one important exception. After cataract surgery with an intraocular lens implant, Medicare Part B pays for one pair of standard frames and lenses (or contact lenses instead). Outside of this post-cataract exception, glasses, frames, lenses, and contact lenses are not a covered Medicare Part B benefit.

The Post-Cataract Surgery Exception

This is the only situation where original Medicare covers corrective lenses. After cataract surgery that includes IOL implantation, Medicare covers one pair of eyeglasses or one set of contact lenses. Coverage includes standard frames from a Medicare-enrolled supplier, prescription lenses (single vision, bifocal, or trifocal), or contact lenses as an alternative to glasses. Medicare pays 80% of the approved amount after the Part B deductible, and the patient pays 20% coinsurance.

Important limitations: upgrades beyond standard frames and lenses (progressive lenses, photochromic lenses, anti-reflective coating, designer frames) are not covered. The patient can choose upgrades but must pay the additional cost out of pocket. The glasses must be obtained from a Medicare-enrolled supplier — not all optical shops are enrolled.

What Medicare Does NOT Cover

  • Routine eyeglasses for refractive error (nearsightedness, farsightedness, astigmatism, presbyopia)
  • Contact lenses except post-cataract or rare medical conditions
  • Lens upgrades beyond standard options
  • Sunglasses — not covered under any circumstance
  • Routine eye exams solely for determining glasses prescription

Medicare Advantage Vision Benefits

Many Medicare Advantage (Part C) plans offer vision benefits that original Medicare does not, including annual routine eye exams, glasses allowances (typically $100-$300 per year), and contact lens allowances. These supplemental vision benefits are one of the primary reasons beneficiaries choose Medicare Advantage. If your patient has an MA plan, always verify their specific vision benefits rather than assuming original Medicare rules apply.

Affordable Alternatives for Medicare Beneficiaries

  • Supplemental vision insurance — Plans from VSP or EyeMed cost $10-$20/month and typically include a glasses allowance
  • Online eyewear retailers — Zenni, EyeBuyDirect, and similar sites offer prescription glasses starting at $15-$50
  • Charitable programs — Lions Club and New Eyes for the Needy provide free glasses for qualifying individuals
  • Warehouse clubs — Costco and Sam's Club optical departments offer competitive pricing on glasses

For ODs: When Medicare patients need glasses but don't qualify for the post-cataract exception, clearly explain that Medicare doesn't cover them. Offer to check whether they have supplemental vision insurance, provide your cash-pay pricing, or refer to affordable options. This transparency builds trust and prevents billing confusion.

The Bottom Line

Medicare's glasses coverage is limited to one specific situation: post-cataract surgery. For everything else, beneficiaries need supplemental vision insurance, Medicare Advantage with vision benefits, or out-of-pocket purchasing. Knowing this distinction — and communicating it clearly to patients — is essential for both ODs and the Medicare beneficiaries they serve.

Does Medicare Cover Optometrists? What ODs Need to Know

By OptometristBillingClarity · April 2026 · 7 min read

Yes — Medicare covers services provided by optometrists. Under Medicare Part B, optometrists (ODs) are recognized as physicians for the purpose of eye care services. This means ODs can evaluate, diagnose, and manage medical eye conditions, perform and interpret diagnostic tests, prescribe medications for eye diseases, and bill Medicare directly for covered services — all without requiring a referral to or supervision by an ophthalmologist.

What Medicare Covers When Provided by an OD

Medicare Part B covers optometrist-provided services including medical eye examinations (92002-92014) for diagnosing and managing eye diseases, diagnostic testing (OCT, visual fields, fundus photography) when medically necessary, glaucoma screening for high-risk patients (annual), diabetic eye exams (annual), post-operative co-management of cataract surgery, and fitting of post-cataract glasses or contacts. Medicare pays the same rates for these services whether they are provided by an optometrist or an ophthalmologist. There is no reimbursement differential based on provider type for the same CPT code.

What Medicare Does NOT Cover From Optometrists

  • Routine eye exams for glasses — Exams solely to determine a glasses prescription are not covered regardless of provider type
  • Refractions (92015) — Not a covered Medicare benefit
  • Routine contact lens fittings — Not covered unless medically necessary (post-cataract, keratoconus)
  • Eyeglasses — Not covered except post-cataract surgery

Scope of Practice Considerations

While Medicare recognizes ODs as physicians for eye care, your scope of practice is determined by your state's optometry practice act — not by Medicare. Medicare will pay for any service that is within your state's scope of practice and within the Medicare-covered benefit. If your state allows ODs to perform certain procedures (laser procedures, injections, minor surgical procedures), Medicare will cover them as long as they are medically necessary and properly coded.

For ODs considering Medicare enrollment: Becoming a Medicare participating provider is straightforward but involves specific steps — NPI registration, PECOS enrollment, and choosing participating vs. non-participating status. Participating providers agree to accept Medicare's allowed amount as full payment. Most OD practices find that participating status is advantageous because the Medicare patient population (65+) represents a significant portion of patients with medical eye conditions.

Medicare Advantage Plans

Medicare Advantage plans must cover at least the same services as original Medicare, but they can add additional benefits (including routine vision exams and glasses allowances). MA plans may require you to be in their provider network. If you see MA patients, you need to credential with each MA plan separately — original Medicare enrollment does not automatically enroll you in MA networks.

Billing at the Same Rate as Ophthalmologists

This is worth emphasizing: Medicare pays ODs and MDs the same amount for the same CPT code. A 92014 billed by an optometrist reimburses identically to a 92014 billed by an ophthalmologist. There is no Medicare discount for optometrist-provided services. This parity means ODs can build financially sustainable practices around Medicare medical eye care without being disadvantaged on reimbursement.

The Bottom Line

Medicare fully covers medical eye care provided by optometrists at the same rates paid to ophthalmologists. ODs are recognized as physicians for eye care under Medicare, can bill directly without referral or supervision, and can provide the full range of medical eye services within their state scope of practice. For ODs building or growing a practice, Medicare patients represent a large, reliable patient base with medical eye conditions that benefit from the ongoing care optometrists provide.
