Medicare Part B Eye Exam Coverage: What Your Chart Must Say to Get Paid

Medicare Part B covers medically necessary eye care — but not routine vision care. For independent optometry practices, that single sentence is the source of more billing denials, post-payment audits, and recoupment demands than any other compliance issue in the specialty.

The problem isn't that ODs are billing incorrectly. The problem is that the chart documentation often doesn't establish medical necessity clearly enough to survive a post-payment review — even when the visit itself was genuinely medically necessary.

This guide explains exactly what Medicare requires, what the most common documentation failures look like, and how independent OD practices can protect themselves from recoupment exposure on CPT codes 92002, 92004, 92012, and 92014.

What Medicare Part B Actually Covers

Medicare Part B covers physician services that are medically necessary — meaning services required to diagnose or treat an illness or injury. For optometry, this means eye exams are covered when there is a medical reason for the visit, not simply because a patient wants a new glasses prescription.

Medicare explicitly excludes routine eye exams — those performed to determine the need for corrective lenses — from Part B coverage. This exclusion is codified in the Social Security Act and enforced by Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs) through their Local Coverage Determinations (LCDs).

"An eye exam is covered by Medicare Part B when the primary purpose of the visit is to diagnose or treat a medical condition of the eye — not to determine refractive error."

The Four CPT Codes at Issue

Independent OD practices billing Medicare Part B primarily use four comprehensive ophthalmological service codes:

  • 92002 — Ophthalmological services, new patient, intermediate
  • 92004 — Ophthalmological services, new patient, comprehensive
  • 92012 — Ophthalmological services, established patient, intermediate
  • 92014 — Ophthalmological services, established patient, comprehensive

All four codes require medical necessity documentation to be covered by Medicare Part B. The distinction between intermediate (92002/92012) and comprehensive (92004/92014) relates to the scope of the examination, not the level of medical necessity required — both require a documented medical reason for the visit.

What Medical Necessity Documentation Must Include

For a Medicare Part B eye exam claim to be defensible, the chart note must clearly establish why the visit was medically necessary. MACs look for specific elements during post-payment reviews:

1. A Medical Chief Complaint

The chart must document a medical symptom or condition — not a vision complaint. Acceptable chief complaints include:

  • Sudden onset of floaters or flashes of light
  • Eye pain, redness, or discharge
  • Blurred vision associated with a known systemic condition (diabetes, hypertension)
  • Follow-up for glaucoma, macular degeneration, or diabetic retinopathy
  • Evaluation for suspected pathology referred by another physician

2. A Documented Medical Diagnosis

The claim must be linked to an ICD-10 diagnosis code that reflects a medical condition — not simply a refractive error. Common medical diagnoses that support Part B coverage include:

  • H40.xx — Glaucoma
  • H35.3x — Macular degeneration
  • E11.3xx — Type 2 diabetes with ophthalmic complications
  • H33.xx — Retinal detachment and breaks
  • H57.xx — Other disorders of eye and adnexa

Documentation Red Flag: If the only diagnosis code on the claim is a refractive error (H52.xx), the claim will likely be denied or flagged for review. A refractive error alone does not establish medical necessity for a Part B-covered exam.

3. Medical Decision-Making That Matches the Code Level

For comprehensive codes (92004/92014), the chart must document a comprehensive examination — including anterior and posterior segment evaluation, confrontation visual fields, and ocular motility. Billing a comprehensive code without documenting comprehensive findings is upcoding — a serious compliance risk.

The Most Common Documentation Failures

Based on MAC audit findings and OIG reports, the most common documentation failures in optometry Medicare billing are:

  • Chief complaint documented as "vision check" or "routine exam" — This language signals routine care, not medical necessity
  • No medical diagnosis linked to the visit — Refractive error codes alone don't support Part B coverage
  • Comprehensive code billed without comprehensive documentation — Upcoding exposure even when visit was medically necessary
  • Follow-up visits not clearly linked to prior medical condition — Each visit must independently establish medical necessity
  • Template notes without patient-specific findings — Cloned documentation is a major audit trigger

The Routine vs. Medical Visit: When a Single Visit Has Both

Many patients present for what begins as a routine exam but also have medical conditions that require evaluation. In these cases, the visit may be bifurcated — with the medical portion billed to Medicare Part B and the routine portion either self-paid or billed to a vision plan.

When bifurcating a visit, the chart must document both components separately, the patient must be given an Advance Beneficiary Notice (ABN) for the routine portion, and the claim must clearly indicate which services are being billed to Part B versus the vision plan.

Incorrect bifurcation — or billing the entire visit to Medicare when only part of it was medically necessary — is one of the most common audit findings in optometry.

How to Protect Your Practice

The most effective protection against Medicare audit exposure on eye exam codes is consistent, specific documentation. Every chart note for a Medicare Part B visit should answer three questions clearly:

  • Why did this patient need a medical eye exam today? (Chief complaint)
  • What medical condition was being evaluated or managed? (Diagnosis)
  • What did the examination find, and what is the plan? (Medical decision-making)

If a post-payment auditor can read your chart note and clearly answer all three questions, your claim is defensible. If any of those three elements is missing or ambiguous, you have exposure.
